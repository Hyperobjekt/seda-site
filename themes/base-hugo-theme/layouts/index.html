We’re measuring educational opportunity in every community in America.

The Stanford Education Data Archive has built the first national database of comparable standardized test scores in the U.S., using more than 330 million test scores. The scores are based on tests administered in grades three through eight from 2009 to 2016 in each public school in the nation.

Standardized test scores tell us three things about how much opportunity students receive:

The average test scores of children in a community reveal the total set of educational opportunities they have had …

from birth to the time they take the tests. The average scores are not good measures of school quality because they are shaped by much more than children’s schools. They reflect not just school-based learning opportunities, but also all the children's opportunities to learn in their homes, in early childhood, in preschool, in their neighborhoods, and from their friends. Many of these out-of-school educational opportunities depend not on school-quality, but on the socioeconomic resources that families have and can use to help their children learn.

The growth of test scores from grade 3 to 8 shows how much students learn on average while they are in school.

They measure the change in students’ knowledge and skills while they are in school, so they reflect the educational opportunities that children have during the years they are in elementary and middle school. They provide useful information about the contribution that schools make in students’ lives, and so are better measures of school quality than average test scores are.

The trend in test scores from 2009 to 2016 indicates the extent to which a community is getting better at providing educational opportunities over time.

For example, rates of improvement tell us whether this year’s third graders are doing better than last year’s third graders. They reflect both changes in school quality and changes in other family and community features that provide opportunities for children.
